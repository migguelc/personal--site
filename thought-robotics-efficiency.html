What Robotics Taught Me About Efficiency and AI

Robotics taught me that intelligence is not the same as excess.

SystemsEfficiencyAI
A reflection on constraint as an engineering advantage.

A robot cannot solve every problem by adding another motor, a larger battery or a more powerful processor. Every gram affects movement. Every millisecond affects control. Every watt affects autonomy. Every additional component creates another potential point of failure.

That discipline changes the way we think. We stop asking, “What else can we add?” and start asking, “What is the simplest system capable of producing the best result?”

This question is becoming essential in artificial intelligence.

The AI conversation is often dominated by scale: more parameters, more data, more GPUs, more agents and more context. Scale has enabled extraordinary progress, but more compute does not automatically produce more understanding.

A weak objective executed faster remains a weak objective. A noisy pipeline with more compute becomes an expensive noisy pipeline. An unclear product powered by a larger model remains an unclear product.

Efficiency is not austerity. It is intelligence expressed through architecture.

This is where I believe Portugal has something important to contribute.

For decades, Portuguese companies, institutions and professionals have learned to compete without the budgets, market size or industrial scale available elsewhere. We became familiar with constraint. We learned to improvise, combine disciplines and make resources travel further.

Sometimes that condition limited us. But it also developed a particular kind of ingenuity: the ability to do the same, or better, with fewer resources.

In AI, constraint can become an engineering philosophy.

It encourages us to improve data before increasing compute. It makes us question unnecessary model calls, fragile agent chains and infrastructure designed for prestige rather than outcomes. It favors systems that are understandable, maintainable and economically deployable.

As access to powerful models becomes more widespread, competitive advantage will move away from simply possessing compute. It will come from framing the right problem, curating the right data, designing the right evaluation and integrating intelligence into a real workflow.

The winners will not always be the teams that spend the most. They may be the teams that waste the least.

Doing more with less should never mean accepting lower standards. In robotics, efficiency is what makes ambitious systems viable. The same is true in AI. Efficient systems can be faster, cheaper, more private and easier to deploy. They can turn an impressive prototype into a sustainable product.

Portugal has the talent, adaptability and human scale to build this kind of AI. We can bring engineers, designers, researchers and domain experts around the same table. We can build for multilingual, cross-border and resource-conscious environments.

Some of the best AI projects in the world can be born here.

Not because Portugal will outspend every larger ecosystem, but because we can learn to outthink waste.

Robotics taught me that the strongest machine is not the one with the most parts. It is the one in which every part has a reason to exist.
